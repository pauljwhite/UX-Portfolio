Making Data Meaningful

Helping Nationwide customers understand the real value of a mortgage overpayment

RoleSenior Experience Designer
TeamUX, Mortgages Product, Legal, Compliance, Engineering
ToolsAxure, Sketch
Timeline2 Months

Overview

At Nationwide Building Society, I designed a data visualisation experience that helped customers understand, in clear and honest terms, how much a mortgage overpayment could save them over the life of their loan. The work sat at the intersection of financial decision-making and regulated communication: every figure shown to a customer had to be accurate, explainable, and strictly reviewed by Legal and Compliance before it could go anywhere near a live product.

The goal was simple to state and hard to deliver. Take numbers that usually live inside spreadsheets and technical calculators, and turn them into something a customer could look at for a few seconds and genuinely understand.

The Problem

Overpayment Felt Abstract

Customers knew that paying more off their mortgage was a good thing in principle, but very few could tell you what a specific overpayment would actually save them. The benefit stayed theoretical, so the action stayed optional.

The Existing Tools Were Intimidating

Calculators on the site expected customers to understand terms like capital, interest, remaining term, and amortisation. For most people the numbers did not land, and the output read more like a mortgage statement than a helpful answer.

A Regulated, High-Trust Conversation

Anything shown to a customer about money, savings, or future benefit had to be rigorously accurate. Illustrative figures could not be misleading, examples had to be explainable, and every piece of copy had to pass legal and compliance review.

A Narrow Window of Attention

Customers were making this decision in passing — on a phone, between other tasks, often without a financial adviser in the room. The design had to communicate a meaningful financial insight in seconds, not minutes.

The Approach

Starting With the Customer Question

Rather than starting with the data model, I started with the question a customer was actually asking: “If I pay a bit more each month, is it really worth it?” Everything in the experience had to answer that plainly. Research sessions with real mortgage customers confirmed that they did not want more numbers; they wanted a straight answer they could trust.

Mapping the Decision, Not the Calculation

I mapped the journey around the moment of decision rather than the mechanics of the calculation. What did a customer need to see first? What would reassure them? What would cause hesitation? This shaped a layout that led with the outcome – the saving – and placed the supporting detail underneath for those who wanted to look deeper.

Designing for Clarity Under Scrutiny

Every element of the visualisation had to be defensible. I worked side by side with Legal, Compliance, and the Mortgages product team from the earliest wireframes, so that nothing was designed in isolation and later rejected. This turned compliance from a gate at the end of the project into a creative constraint from the start.

Honest Visual Hierarchy

The interface used a clear hierarchy: the headline saving first, the assumptions and disclaimers close enough to be part of the same thought, and the underlying breakdown available on demand. Colour and weight were used to signal meaning, never to flatter a number or imply a guarantee.

Plain-English Copy, Reviewed Line by Line

Working closely with a copywriter, I drafted every label, tooltip, and explanation in plain English. Each line was reviewed with Legal and Compliance, with changes tracked and rationalised. Where a technical term was unavoidable, we explained it in context rather than hiding it behind jargon.

Prototyping and Testing With Real Customers

I built interactive prototypes in Axure so the experience could be tested end to end. Customers used the tool with their own mortgage figures and talked through what they were seeing. We iterated on the visualisation until people could read the screen and tell us, unprompted, what an overpayment would save them and why.

Edge Cases and Honest Defaults

We deliberately designed for the edge cases: customers close to the end of their term, customers on fixed deals with early repayment charges, and customers whose overpayment would make only a small difference. The tool had to be as honest in those cases as it was in the flattering ones.

Results

A Clear Answer to a Real Question

Customers could look at the screen and, within seconds, see what an overpayment would save them in both interest and time. In testing, people described the tool as “finally making it make sense” – a direct contrast to the calculator-led experiences that preceded it.

Approved by Legal and Compliance First Time

Because Legal and Compliance were involved from the first sketches, the final design passed formal review without material changes. This was unusual for a piece of regulated financial communication and meaningfully shortened the path to launch.

A Model for Customer-Facing Financial Data

The patterns used here – headline outcome first, honest assumptions in view, deeper detail available on demand – became a reference point for how Nationwide presented other financial decisions to customers, from savings products to term changes.

A Shift in How the Team Worked With Compliance

Perhaps the most lasting outcome was cultural. The project showed product and design teams that regulated copy is not a blocker at the end of a project but a creative constraint at the start. Later work adopted the same practice of reviewing alongside Legal and Compliance from day one.
